Introduction

Central Karakoram National Park (CKNP) is the largest protected area in Pakistan, covering over 10,000 square kilometers in the heart of the Karakoram mountain range in Gilgit-Baltistan. Established in 1993, it is a place of superlatives, home to some of the world's highest peaks, including K2 (8,611m), and the largest glaciers outside the polar regions. It is a world-renowned destination for mountaineering and trekking.

Landscapes & Ecosystems

The park's landscape is defined by extreme vertical relief, with deep valleys and towering granite peaks. It contains over 60 peaks above 7,000 meters. The park is also home to vast glacial systems, including the Baltoro, Biafo, and Hispar glaciers, which are critical sources of fresh water for the region. The ecosystems range from alpine meadows and forests in the lower valleys to barren rock and ice at higher elevations.

Biodiversity And Wildlife

Despite the extreme conditions, CKNP supports a remarkable diversity of wildlife. It is a critical habitat for several endangered species, including the Snow Leopard, Himalayan Brown Bear, and Himalayan Lynx. The park is also home to healthy populations of Himalayan Ibex, Blue Sheep, and Markhor.

Mammals Snow Leopard, Himalayan Brown Bear, Markhor, Marco Polo Sheep, Himalayan Ibex.
Birds Golden Eagle, Lammergeier, Himalayan Snowcock.
Flora Juniper and birch forests in lower valleys, transitioning to alpine scrub and meadows.

Conservation Efforts

Conservation in CKNP focuses on balancing the needs of local communities with the protection of the fragile mountain ecosystem. Efforts include promoting sustainable tourism, managing waste from trekking expeditions, and involving local communities in conservation planning. The park plays a vital role in protecting the watersheds of the Indus River and preserving the unique biodiversity of the Karakoram range.
